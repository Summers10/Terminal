Treasury Yields

3-Month Treasury

DGS3MO

Tracks almost in lockstep with Fed Funds. When the market expects cuts, 3M drops before the Fed moves. Also the "risk-free rate" used in Black-Scholes options pricing.

Desk use: Yield curve anchor. If 3M is above 10Y, the curve is inverted. Front-end proxy for where monetary policy actually is right now.

2-Year Treasury

DGS2

The single most policy-sensitive tenor. Embeds the market's best guess of where Fed Funds will average over the next two years. When 2Y moves sharply, it means the market is repricing the Fed path.

Desk use: Traders watch 2Y on CPI/NFP day more than any other maturity. A 10bp move on a data release is a big deal. Short leg of the 2s10s curve trade.

5-Year Treasury

DGS5

The belly of the curve. Sits between the policy-driven front end and the growth/inflation-driven long end. Approximate duration of mortgage-backed securities portfolios.

Desk use: Key hedging tenor for MBS desks. A clean read on medium-term rate expectations without the term premium distortions that affect the long end.

10-Year Treasury

DGS10

The global benchmark interest rate. Mortgage rates, corporate bond yields, and emerging market borrowing costs all key off the 10Y. Reflects both expected short rates and a term premium for duration risk.

Desk use: Everything references 10Y. Corporate bond spreads are quoted over it. Mortgage rates track it closely. It is the benchmark for essentially all long-term borrowing in the US and globally.

30-Year Treasury

DGS30

The long bond. Most sensitive to long-term inflation expectations and term premium. Pension funds and insurance companies are natural buyers because they need long-duration assets to match liabilities.

Desk use: A "tail" at a 30Y auction (clearing above WI expectations) signals weak demand and can spike yields across the entire curve. Supply/demand dynamics matter most here.

7-Year Treasury

DGS7

Between the belly and the long end. The 7Y auction is historically the weakest-demanded maturity and often tails more than 2s, 5s, or 10s. A useful read on intermediate-term demand.

Desk use: Watch 7Y auction results for demand signals. Often the first tenor to show stress when risk appetite fades. Less liquid than 5s or 10s.
Curve Spreads

10Y minus 2Y (2s10s)

T10Y2Y

The most-watched curve spread. When positive, long rates exceed short rates (normal). When negative (inverted), short rates exceed long rates. Every US recession since the 1970s was preceded by 2s10s inversion, with an average lead time of roughly 11 months.

Desk use: Curve steepeners (long 2s, short 10s) are the canonical trade when expecting Fed cuts. Flatteners for opposite. The danger zone is when the curve normalises from inversion — that typically means recession is arriving and the Fed is about to cut aggressively.

10Y minus 3M

T10Y3M

Similar to 2s10s but uses the very front end. Some economists (notably the NY Fed) prefer this as a recession probability input because 3M is more directly tied to current Fed policy than 2Y, which bakes in forward expectations.

Desk use: Feeds the NY Fed recession probability model. When this goes deeply negative, it means the market sees the current policy rate as significantly above where it will need to be.
Inflation Expectations

5Y Breakeven Inflation

T5YIE

The difference between the 5Y nominal Treasury yield and the 5Y TIPS yield. Represents the market's expectation for average annual CPI inflation over the next 5 years. Rising breakevens = more inflation being priced in.

Desk use: Real-time inflation expectations gauge. If breakevens are rising while nominals are flat, real yields are falling — typically bullish for risk assets and gold. The Fed monitors breakevens to see if expectations are "anchored" near 2%.

10Y Breakeven Inflation

T10YIE

Same concept over a 10-year horizon. Generally more stable than 5Y. The Fed wants this anchored around 2-2.5%. Persistently above 2.5% signals the Fed may be behind the curve on inflation.

Desk use: Comparing 5Y vs 10Y breakevens gives the "5Y5Y forward breakeven" — inflation expectations for the 5-year period starting 5 years from now. This is the Fed's preferred long-term expectations measure.

10Y Real Yield (TIPS Yield)

DFII10

The yield on 10-year TIPS — the inflation-adjusted return from holding a Treasury. This is arguably the "true" cost of capital in the economy. When real yields rise, borrowing becomes genuinely more expensive in purchasing-power terms. Formula: Real yield = Nominal yield - Breakeven inflation.

Desk use: THE single most important driver of risk asset valuations. Higher real yields = higher discount rate = lower equity/gold/crypto valuations. When real yields went from -1% to +2.5% in 2022-2023, it repriced everything. Deeply negative real yields during 2020-2021 were the fuel for the entire risk asset boom.
Fed Policy Rates

Effective Fed Funds Rate

FEDFUNDS

The interest rate at which banks lend reserve balances to each other overnight. The Fed sets a target range (e.g. 4.25-4.50%) and the effective rate trades within it. This is the primary tool of US monetary policy and the anchor for all short-term rates.

Desk use: Everything from SOFR to commercial paper to money market funds is priced relative to Fed Funds. Compare current rate to futures-implied rates to gauge how many cuts or hikes are priced. Note: this FRED series is monthly — use SOFR futures for real-time reads.

SOFR

SOFR

Replaced LIBOR as the benchmark for trillions in floating-rate loans, derivatives, and mortgages. Based on actual overnight Treasury repo transactions, so it reflects real funding costs. Tracks very close to Fed Funds but is market-determined, not set by the Fed.

Desk use: SOFR futures on CME are the primary instrument for trading Fed expectations. "SOFR strip" analysis (looking at consecutive futures contracts) shows exactly where the market expects the Fed to be at each upcoming meeting.
Credit Spreads

ICE BofA High Yield OAS

BAMLH0A0HYM2

The option-adjusted spread of the US high yield (junk bond) index over Treasuries. Measures the extra yield investors demand for holding below-investment-grade corporate debt. Wider spreads = more fear. Tighter = risk-on. Think of it as a real-time fear gauge for corporate credit.

Desk use: One of the best coincident indicators of financial stress. HY spreads blow out before/during recessions — 2008: ~2000bp, Covid: ~1100bp, calm markets: 300-400bp. Sudden widening without an obvious catalyst often signals something is breaking. Tighter than 300bp = euphoric/complacent.

ICE BofA BBB OAS

BAMLC0A4CBBB

The spread for the lowest-quality investment grade bonds (BBB-rated). This is the "cliff edge" — one downgrade below BBB and these bonds become high yield ("fallen angels"), triggering forced selling by funds that can only hold investment grade debt.

Desk use: Monitors the IG/HY boundary. If BBB spreads start widening independently of the broader IG index, it may signal rising downgrade risk. The massive volume of BBB debt outstanding makes this a systemic concern — a wave of downgrades would flood the HY market with supply.
Labor Market

Unemployment Rate

UNRATE

The percentage of the labor force that is unemployed and actively looking for work. Part of the Fed's dual mandate (maximum employment + price stability). The "natural rate" or NAIRU is roughly 4-4.5% — below this tends to be inflationary as employers compete for scarce workers.

Desk use: Released as part of the NFP report. A lagging indicator — unemployment rises AFTER a recession has started. The "Sahm Rule" says recession has begun if the 3-month moving average rises 0.5% above its 12-month low. Rates traders watch for rising unemployment to bring forward Fed cut expectations.

Initial Jobless Claims

ICSA

The number of new unemployment insurance claims filed each week. The most real-time indicator of labor market health available. Unlike NFP (monthly, heavily revised), claims come weekly with a very short lag. A sustained move above 300K historically signals recession risk.

Desk use: The weekly pulse check. Use the 4-week moving average to smooth noise. Steadily rising claims from low levels is the early warning sign. Below 220K = very tight labor market. The key advantage is timeliness — you see deterioration weeks before it shows up in NFP.
Connecting the Dots

Cause & Effect Chains

Everything on this dashboard is interconnected. Here's how the data points interact as a system:

Hot CPI/PCE → breakevens rise, rate cut expectations fall, 2Y yield spikes, curve flattens, credit spreads may widen on growth fears
Weak NFP / rising claims → rate cut expectations rise, 2Y drops, curve steepens (2s10s widens), credit widens on recession risk
Fed cuts rates → front end (3M, 2Y) drops, curve steepens, credit spreads typically tighten, real yields fall
Rising real yields → tighter financial conditions, negative for risk assets, stocks struggle, gold falls, dollar strengthens
Falling real yields → looser financial conditions, positive for risk assets, growth stocks outperform, gold rallies
Credit spreads blowout → risk-off, flight to quality into Treasuries (yields fall), curve bull flattens, equities sell off
Curve normalising from inversion → historically the danger zone — often means recession is arriving and the Fed is about to cut aggressively

Inflation & Liquidity

CPI (All Urban Consumers)

CPALTT01USM657N

The headline consumer price index. Shown as year-over-year % change. The single most important inflation print for markets — drives rate expectations, TIPS pricing, and FOMC rhetoric.

Desk use: CPI day is the highest-vol macro event alongside NFP. Core CPI (ex food/energy) matters more for Fed policy, but headline moves commodity markets directly since it includes food and energy. A hot print lifts the dollar and pressures grains.

M2 Money Supply

M2SL

Broad money supply — cash, checking, savings, money market funds. The explosive M2 growth in 2020-2021 (~40% above trend) was a leading indicator of the inflation surge. Now contracting/flat as QT continues.

Desk use: M2 growth vs nominal GDP growth is a rough inflation pressure gauge. Excess M2 above trend had to either be absorbed by growth or show up as inflation. Watch for re-acceleration as a signal that liquidity conditions are loosening again.

Fed Balance Sheet (WALCL)

WALCL

Total assets held by the Federal Reserve — Treasuries, MBS, and lending facilities. QE expands it (adds reserves/liquidity), QT shrinks it. Peaked near $9T in 2022, now running off at ~$60B/month.

Desk use: The balance sheet is the "second lever" of monetary policy alongside rates. When the Fed stops QT or restarts QE, it's a major liquidity signal. Watch for sudden jumps (emergency lending) as crisis indicators. Commodity traders watch it as a USD liquidity proxy.
